Starter care in plain English: how to start one, how to feed it, when to use it, and when to leave it alone.

Starter 101

What starter actually is

A sourdough starter is flour and water that has been colonized by wild yeast and lactic acid bacteria. The yeast helps bread rise. The bacteria help create acidity and flavor. That is the whole system. Useful, alive, and much less mysterious once you know what you are looking at.

Most recipes on this site assume a 100% hydration starter, which means equal weights of flour and water. Not equal cups. Equal grams. Cups are too inconsistent for starter math.

Start a Starter

Starting a starter from scratch

A new starter is just flour, water, and time. You are creating a small culture and feeding it until it becomes predictable. Predictable matters more than dramatic bubbles on day two.

Reality check: most new starters take about 7 to 14 days before they are reliable enough for bread. Some take longer. Early activity does not automatically mean it is ready.

Basic starter build

Use a clean jar, a spoon, a scale, flour, and water. Whole wheat or rye can help get things moving, but regular unbleached flour can work too.

When it is ready to bake with

Your starter is ready for bread when it reliably doubles after feeding, has visible bubbles, and follows a repeatable rise-and-fall pattern. A good target is doubling within roughly 4 to 8 hours after a normal feeding at comfortable room temperature.

If it only doubled once, give it more time. One good day is encouraging. A pattern is useful.

When to discard

Discard during feedings so the jar does not become an expensive flour bucket. Keep a small amount of starter, then feed that small amount with fresh flour and water. The discard can be saved for discard recipes once the starter is established, but very young discard may be harsh and unpredictable.

When to switch to maintenance

Once the starter is rising predictably, stop treating it like a science fair project. Keep a smaller amount, feed it on the counter if you bake often, or store it in the fridge if you bake less often.

Feeding

What feeding means

Feeding starter means keeping some starter and adding fresh flour and water. The old starter is the seed. The new flour and water are the food.

Starter20g
Flour60g
Water60g

That example is a 1:3:3 feed: one part starter, three parts flour, three parts water. It makes about 140g total starter before normal loss from starter sticking to the jar and spoon.

Maintenance feed: the routine feed that keeps your starter alive. Baking feed: the bigger, timed feed that gives you enough active starter for a recipe plus some left over to keep.

Ratios

Ratios control timing and flavor

A smaller feed, like 1:1:1, gives the starter less fresh food, so it peaks faster and tends to be more acid-forward. A larger feed, like 1:5:5 or 1:10:10, gives it more fresh food, so it usually takes longer to peak and tends to taste milder.

Hydration

Hydration is just water math

Hydration means how much water is in the dough compared with the flour. It is written as a percentage because bakers like making normal things sound like tax forms.

The basic formula: water weight divided by flour weight, then multiplied by 100. So 350g water and 500g flour is 70% hydration.

Starter hydration

This site assumes a 100% hydration starter, which means the starter is fed equal weights flour and water. Example: 20g starter, 60g flour, and 60g water. That keeps the math predictable.

If you use a stiff starter or a very liquid starter, the recipes may still work, but the dough will not behave exactly the same. Different starter hydration changes the total flour and water in the dough.

Dough hydration

Higher hydration dough has more water. It usually feels wetter, spreads more easily, and can be harder to shape. Lower hydration dough feels firmer and is usually easier to handle.

  • Lower hydration: easier handling, tighter dough, often a simpler place to start.
  • Higher hydration: wetter dough, more sticking, more folding and handling skill needed.
  • Whole grain, rye, cocoa, seeds, and some add-ins: often absorb more water, so the same hydration can feel different from one recipe to another.

Do not chase numbers for sport

A higher hydration number does not automatically mean better bread. It just means more water. Start with a manageable dough, learn how it behaves, then adjust from there.

Adjustment rule: change water in small amounts, usually 5g to 15g at a time, and write it down. Random splashing is not a system.

Active vs discard

Use the right version of the goo

Active starter

Starter that has been fed, has risen, and is at or near peak. Use this when the starter is responsible for making dough rise.

Discard

Unfed or past-peak starter removed before feeding. Use it in discard recipes for flavor, acidity, and less waste, not as your main lift unless the recipe says so.

If a recipe says active starter, do not casually swap in flat discard. Active starter and discard do different jobs.

Peak

Peak is the useful window

Peak is when your starter has risen as high as it is going to rise before it starts sinking back down. For bread, that is usually the best time to mix it into dough because the starter is strong and active.

  • Before peak: it is still rising and may not be fully ready.
  • At peak: it is usually bubbly, expanded, and strong enough for baking.
  • After peak: it starts collapsing. It can still be useful, but it may bring more acidity and less lift.
Use the starter, not the clock, as the truth. The clock gives you a plan. The jar tells you what actually happened.

Temperature

Temperature is the schedule boss

Starter and dough move faster when they are warm and slower when they are cool. That is why sourdough timing is always an estimate. The recipe can give you a plan, but the room gets a vote.

Starter planningabout 70°F
Dough target75–78°F
Baked loaf200–210°F

Where to put starter or dough when the kitchen is cool

You are looking for gently warm, not hot. A few useful places to test:

Top of the fridge

Often a little warmer than the counter. Good for starter builds and slow dough.

Counter over the dishwasher

Useful after the dishwasher has run, especially if the kitchen runs cold.

Warm dryer

Use only the leftover warmth after the dryer is off. Do not run the dryer with dough in it. Bread does not need tumble dry low.

Other warm spot

Try a microwave with a mug of hot water, an oven with just the light on, or a proofing box if you have one.

Check the actual temperature. Warm spots can be warmer than you think. If it feels hot, it is too hot for starter or dough. Move it somewhere gentler.

Using a thermometer

A thermometer is one of the easiest ways to stop guessing. Use it to check dough temperature during fermentation and internal temperature after baking.

  • For starter: temperature helps explain timing, but readiness is still based on rise, bubbles, and a predictable peak.
  • For dough: a target dough temperature around 75–78°F keeps many basic sourdough schedules moving predictably.
  • For baked loaves: lean sourdough bread is generally done when the center is about 200–210°F and the crust is browned.
  • For enriched or specialty recipes: follow the recipe. English muffins, for example, are usually done around 200°F in the center.

Storage

Counter or fridge?

If you bake often, keeping a small starter on the counter makes sense. Feed it regularly and keep the amount small so you are not creating a discard swamp.

If you bake less often, keep it in the fridge. Cold slows fermentation, which means less feeding and less waste. When you want to bake, pull it out, feed it, and give it time to become active again. A neglected fridge starter may need more than one feeding before it is ready to do actual work.

  • Small counter routine: keep 10g to 20g starter, then feed equal weights flour and water for maintenance.
  • Timed baking feed: build only what the recipe needs plus the starter you want left over.
  • Fridge rule: cold starter is convenient, not instantly ready. Wake it up before asking it to raise bread.

Variables

Things that change the plan

Sourdough timing is not one clean little number. It changes because the starter is alive and your kitchen conditions matter.

  • Warm room: faster fermentation.
  • Cool room: slower fermentation.
  • Rye or whole grain flour: often more active, but also absorbs more water.
  • Sugar, honey, or fruit in dough: can speed fermentation.
  • Cinnamon, garlic, fat, and eggs: can slow fermentation.
  • Cocoa, rye, whole grains, and seeds: can absorb more water, so the dough may need hydration adjustments.

Common sense rules

Starter care without making it complicated

  • Use grams. A scale is more consistent than measuring cups.
  • Keep less starter. You can always build more when you need it.
  • Do not use moldy starter. Pink, orange, or fuzzy growth means stop being optimistic and throw it out.
  • Do not panic over hooch, the dark liquid that can collect on hungry starter. It usually means the starter is hungry. Stir it in or pour it off, then feed.
  • Do not treat peak windows as law. They are estimates. Your kitchen conditions can change the timing.
  • When testing changes, change one thing at a time. Otherwise you will not know what actually worked.
