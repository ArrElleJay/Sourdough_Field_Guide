How to use this

Recipe plans live with the recipes.

Each recipe has its own baking plan because each recipe behaves differently. A loaf, a cinnamon roll, a bagel, and a cracker should not be forced into the same timeline.

  • Use the recipe page when you need the actual ingredients and method.
  • Use Quick recipe version when you want the short kitchen view.
  • Use Baking plan when you want the printable checklist for your counter.

Standard loaf rhythm

For a normal active-starter loaf

  • Feed starter the night before or early morning, depending on ratio and room temperature.
  • Mix when the starter is active and predictable.
  • Rest 30 minutes after mixing.
  • Do stretch and folds on the recipe schedule.
  • Bulk until the dough has clearly risen and feels alive.
  • Shape, proof, bake, then cool before slicing.

Overnight rhythm

For less babysitting

  • Feed starter during the day.
  • Mix dough in the evening.
  • Use the fridge when the dough needs to wait for you.
  • Bake the next morning or later the next day, depending on the recipe.

Fridge holds

The fridge is a pause button, mostly.

Cold slows fermentation. It does not erase it. Use the fridge when the dough is ready but you are not, or when a recipe specifically calls for an overnight hold.

Temperature

Warm moves faster. Cool moves slower.

Timing changes with room temperature, starter strength, flour, hydration, and how warm the dough is after mixing. Use the clock as a plan, then let the dough have the final word.

Same-day backup plan

Bread emergency

This is the compressed same-day loaf plan. It assumes a strong starter, a warm starter feed, warm bulk fermentation, and the goal of getting bread out of the oven around 3:00–3:30 PM so it can cool for a normal dinner.

Reality check: this is a rescue plan, not the best possible loaf. If the starter is sluggish or the dough is not moving, dinner bread becomes later bread.
