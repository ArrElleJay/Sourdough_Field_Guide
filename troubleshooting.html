What Had Happened Was...

The bread did something weird.

Start with the obvious stuff first. Temperature, timing, starter strength, and dough handling explain most sourdough drama.

Use this page as a diagnosis guide, not a courtroom. One bad loaf does not mean the recipe failed, your starter is dead, or you need to buy three new tools. Check the likely causes, adjust one thing next time, and keep notes.

Starter Dough Proofing Baking Recipe-specific

First, check the boring things.

  • Was the starter actually at peak, or just bubbly enough to lie to you?
  • Was the dough warm, cool, or sitting in a kitchen with opinions?
  • Did the dough rise because it was ready, or because the clock said it should be?
  • Did you change flour, hydration, temperature, add-ins, or timing?
  • Did you cut into the bread before it cooled? Be honest.

Starter problems

The jar is not cooperating.

Starter is not rising

Likely cause
It is too cool, underfed, recently neglected, or not established yet.
What to do now
Move it somewhere warmer and feed 1:1:1 for a few cycles. Mark the level on the jar so you can tell if it actually rises.
Next time
Do not ask a sluggish starter to carry a loaf. Wait until it rises predictably after feeding.

Starter peaked and collapsed

Likely cause
It ate through the food and ran out of steam before you used it.
What to do now
If it only recently dropped, it may still work, but expect more sourness and less lift. For a stronger bake, feed again and use at peak.
Next time
Use a higher feeding ratio if you need a longer wait before mixing.

Starter has liquid on top

Likely cause
It is hungry or has been sitting too long. The liquid is usually hooch.
What to do now
Stir it in for more sour flavor or pour it off for less. Feed normally and watch how it responds.
Next time
Feed sooner, use a larger feed, or store it in the fridge if you are not baking often.

New starter bubbled, then went quiet

Likely cause
Normal early-starter behavior. The first burst does not mean it is ready.
What to do now
Keep feeding. Most new starters need days of predictable rise and fall before bread duty.
Next time
Judge by consistency, not one dramatic bubble day.

Dough problems

The dough is being a problem.

Dough is too sticky

Likely cause
High hydration, warm dough, weak flour, not enough gluten development, or too much fermentation.
What to do now
Wet your hands, use a bench scraper, and give the dough time. Avoid dumping in flour unless it is truly unmanageable.
Next time
Hold back a little water, use stronger flour, or shorten warm fermentation.

Dough spreads instead of holding shape

Likely cause
Weak gluten, overproofing, too much hydration, or shaping without enough tension.
What to do now
Use a tighter shape and support it in a banneton, bowl, or loaf pan if needed.
Next time
Add structure with folds, watch bulk more closely, and do not let warm dough run the whole show.

Dough is not rising

Likely cause
Starter was weak, dough is too cool, salt was accidentally overmeasured, or the formula is enriched and moving slowly.
What to do now
Move it warmer and give it more time. If it has not moved at all after a long wait, the starter may not have been ready.
Next time
Use starter at peak and check dough temperature early.

Dough tore during shaping

Likely cause
It was too tight, under-rested, dry on the surface, or handled too aggressively.
What to do now
Let it rest 10 minutes, then shape more gently.
Next time
Cover dough during rests and give it time to relax before final shaping.

Proofing problems

The clock lied. It does that.

Dense loaf

Likely cause
Under-fermented dough, weak starter, or not enough proofing before bake.
What to do now
There is no magic after baking. Toast it, cube it, or call it rustic and move on.
Next time
Let bulk go further, use a warmer spot, and make sure your starter is reliably active.

Gummy crumb

Likely cause
Underbaking, slicing too soon, under-fermentation, or too much hydration for the flour.
What to do now
Let the loaf cool fully. If it is already cut, toast slices to drive off extra moisture.
Next time
Bake to temperature, cool longer, and watch fermentation more than the clock.

Huge holes with dense areas

Likely cause
Uneven shaping, trapped air pockets, or fermentation that was not balanced.
What to do now
Still edible. Probably toast-worthy.
Next time
Shape more evenly and degas giant bubbles while keeping the dough gentle.

Loaf flattened after scoring

Likely cause
Overproofed dough, weak shaping, or dough that was too warm and slack.
What to do now
Bake it anyway. Flat bread is still bread.
Next time
Shorten final proof or use the fridge to firm the dough before scoring.
Useful rule: warmer dough moves faster. Cooler dough moves slower. If your kitchen is not close to the recipe’s assumed temperature, the timeline will not match perfectly.

Baking problems

The oven had its own agenda.

Bottom burned

Likely cause
Too much heat from the bottom, thin Dutch oven base, low oven rack, or too long on the hot surface.
What to do now
Trim the worst of it if needed. The rest is probably fine.
Next time
Move the rack higher, put a sheet pan under the Dutch oven, or remove the loaf from the Dutch oven after baking.

Crust is too hard

Likely cause
Too much uncovered bake, not enough steam, or a loaf that baked too dry.
What to do now
Let it cool under a towel if you want the crust to soften slightly.
Next time
Keep the covered bake long enough, shorten the uncovered bake, or check oven temperature.

Loaf is pale

Likely cause
Not enough uncovered bake, oven too cool, or dough lacking enough fermentation for browning.
What to do now
Put it back in for a few minutes if the loaf is not fully cooled and the inside is done.
Next time
Preheat fully and give the uncovered phase enough time.

Loaf burst somewhere weird

Likely cause
Scoring was too shallow, skin tension was uneven, or the loaf needed a better escape route.
What to do now
Call it character.
Next time
Score decisively and shape with more even surface tension.

Recipe-specific problems

Not every recipe fails the same way.

Bagels are dry or tough

Likely cause
Low hydration, over-kneading, too much whole grain/cocoa/rye without adjusting water, or boiling too long.
What to do now
They may still be excellent toasted.
Next time
Add a little water for thirsty flours and keep the boil to the recipe’s timing.

Crackers are not crisp

Likely cause
Dough was rolled too thick, crackers were crowded, or they needed more drying time.
What to do now
Return them to a low oven or the turned-off oven to dry out more.
Next time
Roll thinner than you think you need to. Annoyingly thin.

Cinnamon rolls did not puff

Likely cause
Cold dough, weak starter, underproofing, or enriched dough moving slower because of milk, butter, egg, and sugar.
What to do now
Give them more time somewhere warm before baking.
Next time
Plan for the second rise to take the full window, especially if the dough was chilled.

English muffins are doughy inside

Likely cause
Pan was too hot, outside browned before the center finished, or muffins were too thick.
What to do now
Split and toast them. That fixes a lot.
Next time
Cook low and covered, and check the center with a thermometer if needed.
